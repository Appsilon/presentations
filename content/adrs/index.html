What is Architecture Decision?

What is AD?

An Architectural Decision (AD) is a software design choice that addresses a functional or non-functional requirement that is architecturally significant.

What is Architecture Decision Record?

What is ADR?

An Architectural Decision Record (ADR) captures a single AD, such as often done when writing personal notes or meeting minutes; the collection of ADRs created and maintained in a project constitutes its decision log.

ADR Template

So, let's take a look at a what a typical template would look like in ADR's world. And full disclosure of course: ADRs is just a concept, there's a bunch of templates available that can fit various project, from small to larger ones. The template I personally find the most intuitive and easy to use is the one suggested my Michael Nygard in 2011, which interestingly enough is 4 year ahead the article from Google. Conceptually, the document structure is similar to one we've seen so far. But let's take a closer look directly in the Markdown itself.

ADR Template in Markdown


            # Title

            ## Status
            What is the status, such as proposed, accepted, rejected, deprecated, superseded, etc.?

            ## Context
            What is the issue that we're seeing that is motivating this decision or change?

            ## Decision
            ..
  • The document starts with the "Title", short but discoverable label
  • Status: a meta field, the status of the work described, a dated list of status changes works as well
  • Context: what's the problem? What's the effect? Why did this problem was raised? Facts, not assumptions or opinions.
  • Decision: what should happen in order to mitigate the issue? How is success defined? What further recommendations are there? What are alternative solutions?
  • Consequences: the resulting context, either good or bad. Basically, the effect of the work described

            # Title

            ## Status
            What is the status, such as proposed, accepted, rejected, deprecated, superseded, etc.?

            ## Context
            What is the issue that we're seeing that is motivating this decision or change?

            ## Decision
            What is the change that we're proposing and/or doing?

            ## Consequences
            What becomes easier or more difficult to do because of this change?

ADR In a nutshell

So, in a nutshell, and ADR can be treated as a very lightweight document consisting of decision taken, it might include external links and reference to third party documents whenever required, but at the same time the document itself stays compact and to-the-point. It might also live anywhere, in any format. Markdown is a preference though. A bit of a spoiler, since some of you might have guessed, the ideal place to store ADRs would be your project's repository.

ADRs should be considered as lightweight records of decision making, linking in other documents and content when required, keeping ADR itself compact and to-the-point. It can live anywhere, in any format.

Who writes ADRs and why?

  • ADRs can and should be written by any engineer in the team. They work best when decision are taken mutually
  • They should be considered as living documents, providing useful documentation throughout the life of a project.
  • They provide a common framework for the communication of technical decisions, which allows engineers of all levels a consistent way of influencing strategy.
  • Having a clear, supportive process around ADRs allows the democratization of architecture, stronger team alignment, and an effective foundation for advertising various engineering concepts and best practices as well.

Tooling

As always when some kind of a standard is being introduced, there's also a set of tools to interact with it. ADRs are not an exception and there's a plenty of open source CLI and web applications on the market that can assist you in your day-to-day work when writing documentation. The first one is kinda simple - it's a plain bash helper that manages the ADR Markdown files on your behalf, simplifying things like bootstrapping, adding or removing existing documents. The second one is a more sophisticated tool written in TypeScript, which not only has the same features as adr-tools, but also works as a static website publisher, allowing you to share and manage your documents in a more human-friendly way.
  • adr-tools - bash scripts to manage ADRs in the Nygard format
    •
  • log4brains - CLI and web UI to log and publish your ADRs as a static website
